You each approach wildfire from a fundamentally different angle — AI and data systems, atmospheric fluid dynamics, and fire ecology. What is the specific piece of the wildfire prediction chain that your work addresses?

Question 02
How does North Florida's specific character shape your research?
North Florida's wildfire environment — its humid climate, longleaf pine ecosystem, and deeply ingrained prescribed burn culture — is fundamentally different from the western U.S. or Mediterranean regions. How does this region's specific character shape your methods, data needs, or assumptions?
Grounds the panel in the local context. Signals North Florida as a scientifically distinct environment.

Question 03
How do errors resulting from uncertainty affect your predictions?
In your research, where does uncertainty primarily come from — insufficient input data, inherent unpredictability in physical or ecological processes, or both? How do you quantify, manage, or communicate the errors that result?
A technically rich question each panelist answers from their own domain. Reveals the honest limits of current science.

Question 04
How do human activities alter the landscape your models rely on?
Human activities — such as prescribed burning, land-use change, and urban expansion — continuously reshape fuel structure, fire behavior patterns, and the historical record that models are trained on. How does your research account for and adapt to these ongoing changes?
Broadens the question to human land management at large. Relevant across all three panelists' domains.

Question 05
What has AI made newly possible — and what do you worry it's oversimplifying?
Large-scale remote sensing and AI have made some previously unanswerable questions tractable. What is one question your field can now begin to answer that it couldn't five years ago — and is there anything you worry is being oversimplified by the current wave of AI enthusiasm?
Celebrates genuine progress while naming what could be lost. Directly relevant to AI researchers and students in the room.

Question 01
What stage of wildfire response is most central to your work?
Whether you focus on pre-event planning, real-time decision-making, or post-incident recovery — which stage do you work in most closely, and what kinds of decisions or actions does that involve?
Grounds each panelist's role in the response timeline. Sets up all subsequent questions with a shared sense of where each person stands.

Question 02

In your day-to-day work, what information or data do you rely on most — where does it come from, how reliable is it, and what is typically missing or difficult to obtain when it matters?
Surfaces the real information gaps practitioners face. Directly useful to researchers who may not know what is operationally missing.

Question 03
What research outputs or tools — such as AI models — have been difficult to translate into operational use?
Research regularly produces tools, models, and findings intended to support decision-making in the field. Where have these fallen short in practice — whether due to format, timing, accessibility, or how uncertainty is communicated?
Directly addresses the science-to-practice gap at the core of this workshop. Invites candid answers about where the bridge breaks down.
Q 04

These Rules
Region-Specific Context
click to reveal
Question 04
What aspects of this region's wildfire context require approaches developed specifically for it?
North Florida has one of the most active prescribed burn cultures in the country, a multi-county regional planning structure, and a Wildland-Urban Interface that differs substantially from California or the Pacific Northwest. What aspects of your work here cannot be directly informed by frameworks or methods developed for other regions?
Localizes the panel. Draws on knowledge only practitioners embedded in this region would have.

Question 05
Where does the system currently fail the people most at risk?
Wildfire evacuation and emergency response are not equally accessible to everyone. In your specific work — whether modeling evacuation behavior, planning across nine counties, or coordinating first response — where does the system fall short for people with less mobility, fewer resources, or less access to information?
Brings an equity lens in a concrete, experience-grounded way. Each panelist names a different dimension of the gap.

Question 06
What should researchers stop building — and what would actually change your work?
Speaking directly to the researchers in this room: what is one thing the research community keeps building that doesn't actually help you — and what is one thing that, if someone built it well, would immediately change how you work?
The most pointed question of the workshop. Lets practitioners give unfiltered feedback to researchers — the perfect closing for a Bridging-themed session.
